How can you compute \(\operatorname{tr}(\boldsymbol{A})\) exactly using standard basis vectors?	\(\operatorname{tr}(\boldsymbol{A}) = \sum_{i=1}^n \boldsymbol{e}_i^*\boldsymbol{A}\boldsymbol{e}_i\), which requires \(n\) matrix-vector products with the standard basis vectors \(\boldsymbol{e}_1, \ldots, \boldsymbol{e}_n\).	rand_linal::trace_estimation
What condition must a random vector \(\boldsymbol{\omega} \sim \mathcal{D}\) satisfy for \(\mathbb{E}[\boldsymbol{\omega}^*\boldsymbol{A}\boldsymbol{\omega}] = \operatorname{tr}(\boldsymbol{A})\)?	The random vector must be <b>isotropic</b>: \(\mathbb{E}[\boldsymbol{\omega}\boldsymbol{\omega}^*] = \boldsymbol{I}_n\).	rand_linal::trace_estimation
What is the Girard-Hutchinson estimator for \(\operatorname{tr}(\boldsymbol{A})\)?	Draw \(m\) independent isotropic random vectors \(\boldsymbol{\omega}_i \sim \mathcal{D}\), then estimate \(\operatorname{tr}(\boldsymbol{A}) \approx \frac{1}{m}\sum_{i=1}^m \boldsymbol{\omega}_i^*\boldsymbol{A}\boldsymbol{\omega}_i\). This requires only \(m \ll n\) matrix-vector products.	rand_linal::trace_estimation
Prove that \(\mathbb{E}[\boldsymbol{w}^\top \boldsymbol{A} \boldsymbol{w}] = \operatorname{tr}(\boldsymbol{A})\) using the trace-of-scalar trick.	(1) \(\boldsymbol{w}^\top \boldsymbol{A} \boldsymbol{w}\) is a scalar, so \(\boldsymbol{w}^\top \boldsymbol{A} \boldsymbol{w} = \operatorname{tr}(\boldsymbol{w}^\top \boldsymbol{A} \boldsymbol{w})\). (2) Cyclic property: \(\operatorname{tr}(\boldsymbol{w}^\top \boldsymbol{A} \boldsymbol{w}) = \operatorname{tr}(\boldsymbol{A} \boldsymbol{w} \boldsymbol{w}^\top)\). (3) Linearity of expectation and trace: \(\mathbb{E}[\operatorname{tr}(\boldsymbol{A} \boldsymbol{w} \boldsymbol{w}^\top)] = \operatorname{tr}(\boldsymbol{A} \mathbb{E}[\boldsymbol{w} \boldsymbol{w}^\top]) = \operatorname{tr}(\boldsymbol{A} \boldsymbol{I}) = \operatorname{tr}(\boldsymbol{A})\).	rand_linal::trace_estimation
What does it mean for a set of random vectors to be in <b>isotropic position</b>?	A set of vectors \(\mathcal{D}\) is in <b>isotropic position</b> if for every vector \(\boldsymbol{w} \in \mathcal{D}\), we have \(\mathbb{E}[\boldsymbol{w}\boldsymbol{w}^\top] = \boldsymbol{I}\). Such vectors are called <b>isotropic</b>.	rand_linal::trace_estimation
What distribution did Hutchinson propose for the Girard-Hutchinson estimator, and what is its optimality property?	Hutchinson proposed using <b>Rademacher random vectors</b> (entries \(\pm 1\) with equal probability). This choice <b>minimizes the variance</b> of the estimator when \(\boldsymbol{A}\) is fixed.	rand_linal::trace_estimation
What distribution did Girard propose, and what is its optimality property?	Girard proposed the <b>uniform distribution over the \(\ell_2\) hypersphere</b> with radius \(\sqrt{n}\). This choice minimizes the <b>worst-case variance</b> over sets of matrices closed under conjugation by unitary matrices: \(\boldsymbol{A} \in S \implies \boldsymbol{U}\boldsymbol{A}\boldsymbol{U}^* \in S\).	rand_linal::trace_estimation
How many samples does the basic Girard-Hutchinson estimator require for \(\epsilon\) error?	It requires \(m \in \Omega(1/\epsilon^2)\) samples. This is characteristic of Monte Carlo methods: to decrease error by a factor of 10, you need \(100\times\) the work.	rand_linal::trace_estimation
What is the variance of the Girard-Hutchinson estimator with \(m\) iid isotropic samples?	\(\operatorname{Var}(\hat{\operatorname{tr}}) = \frac{1}{m^2}\sum_{i=1}^m \operatorname{Var}(\boldsymbol{w}_i^* \boldsymbol{A} \boldsymbol{w}_i) = \frac{1}{m}\operatorname{Var}(\boldsymbol{w}^* \boldsymbol{A} \boldsymbol{w})\). The variance decreases like \(1/m\), giving root-mean-square error decay of \(1/\sqrt{m}\).	rand_linal::trace_estimation
What is the Compress-and-Trace method for trace estimation?	(1) Use a rangefinder to find a column-orthonormal \(n \times m\) matrix \(\boldsymbol{Q}\) where \(\boldsymbol{Q}\boldsymbol{Q}^*\boldsymbol{A}\boldsymbol{Q}\boldsymbol{Q}^* \approx \boldsymbol{A}\). (2) Approximate \(\operatorname{tr}(\boldsymbol{A}) \approx \operatorname{tr}(\boldsymbol{Q}\boldsymbol{Q}^*\boldsymbol{A}\boldsymbol{Q}\boldsymbol{Q}^*) = \operatorname{tr}(\boldsymbol{Q}^*\boldsymbol{A}\boldsymbol{Q})\), reducing to an \(m \times m\) trace computation.	rand_linal::compress_and_trace
Why does \(\operatorname{tr}(\boldsymbol{Q}\boldsymbol{Q}^*\boldsymbol{A}\boldsymbol{Q}\boldsymbol{Q}^*) = \operatorname{tr}(\boldsymbol{Q}^*\boldsymbol{A}\boldsymbol{Q})\)?	By the cyclic property of trace: \(\operatorname{tr}(\boldsymbol{Q}\boldsymbol{Q}^*\boldsymbol{A}\boldsymbol{Q}\boldsymbol{Q}^*) = \operatorname{tr}(\boldsymbol{Q}^*\boldsymbol{Q}\boldsymbol{Q}^*\boldsymbol{A}\boldsymbol{Q}) = \operatorname{tr}(\boldsymbol{Q}^*\boldsymbol{A}\boldsymbol{Q})\), since \(\boldsymbol{Q}^*\boldsymbol{Q} = \boldsymbol{I}_m\) (columns of \(\boldsymbol{Q}\) are orthonormal).	rand_linal::compress_and_trace
How can trace estimation be applied when \(\boldsymbol{A} = \log(\boldsymbol{I} + \boldsymbol{B})\) for PSD \(\boldsymbol{B}\)?	Find \(\boldsymbol{Q}\) so that \(\boldsymbol{Q}\boldsymbol{Q}^*\boldsymbol{B}\boldsymbol{Q}\boldsymbol{Q}^*\) is a good low-rank approximation of \(\boldsymbol{B}\), then \(\operatorname{tr}(\boldsymbol{A}) \approx \sum_{i=1}^m \log(1 + \lambda_i(\boldsymbol{Q}^*\boldsymbol{B}\boldsymbol{Q}))\), where \(\lambda_i(\cdot)\) are the eigenvalues of the compressed matrix.	rand_linal::compress_and_trace
State and prove: for PSD matrix \(\boldsymbol{M}\), \(\operatorname{tr}(\log \boldsymbol{M}) = \log(\det \boldsymbol{M})\).	<b>Proof:</b> Let \(\boldsymbol{M} = \boldsymbol{Q}\boldsymbol{\Lambda}\boldsymbol{Q}^*\) (eigendecomposition). <b>RHS:</b> \(\log(\det \boldsymbol{M}) = \log(\prod \lambda_i) = \sum \log \lambda_i = \operatorname{tr}(\log \boldsymbol{\Lambda})\). <b>LHS:</b> \(\log \boldsymbol{M} = \boldsymbol{Q}\log(\boldsymbol{\Lambda})\boldsymbol{Q}^*\), so \(\operatorname{tr}(\log \boldsymbol{M}) = \operatorname{tr}(\boldsymbol{Q}\log(\boldsymbol{\Lambda})\boldsymbol{Q}^*) = \operatorname{tr}(\log(\boldsymbol{\Lambda})\boldsymbol{Q}^*\boldsymbol{Q}) = \operatorname{tr}(\log \boldsymbol{\Lambda})\). Both sides equal \(\sum \log \lambda_i\).	rand_linal::compress_and_trace
What is the role of the matrix \(\boldsymbol{Q} \in \mathbb{R}^{n \times m}\) in matrix compression for trace estimation?	\(\boldsymbol{Q}\) is a tall orthonormal matrix (\(m \ll n\)) whose columns approximately span the dominant eigenspace of \(\boldsymbol{A}\). Then \(\boldsymbol{Q}\boldsymbol{Q}^*\boldsymbol{A}\boldsymbol{Q}\boldsymbol{Q}^*\) is a rank-\(m\) approximation, and \(\boldsymbol{Q}^*\boldsymbol{A}\boldsymbol{Q}\) is the small \(m \times m\) compressed matrix with the same trace.	rand_linal::compress_and_trace
Describe the four steps of the Hutch++ algorithm.	(1) Sample \(\boldsymbol{Q}\) uniformly from the set of \(n \times m\) column-orthonormal matrices. (2) Compute the low-rank trace: \(\operatorname{tr}(\hat{\boldsymbol{A}}) = \operatorname{tr}(\boldsymbol{Q}^*\boldsymbol{A}\boldsymbol{Q})\). (3) Apply Girard-Hutchinson to the <b>deflated matrix</b> \(\boldsymbol{\Delta} = (\boldsymbol{I} - \boldsymbol{Q}\boldsymbol{Q}^*)\boldsymbol{A}(\boldsymbol{I} - \boldsymbol{Q}\boldsymbol{Q}^*)\). (4) Combine: \(\operatorname{tr}(\boldsymbol{A}) = \operatorname{tr}(\hat{\boldsymbol{A}}) + \operatorname{tr}(\boldsymbol{\Delta})\).	rand_linal::hutch_pp
In Hutch++, what is the deflated matrix \(\boldsymbol{\Delta}\) and what does it represent?	\(\boldsymbol{\Delta} = (\boldsymbol{I} - \boldsymbol{Q}\boldsymbol{Q}^*)\boldsymbol{A}(\boldsymbol{I} - \boldsymbol{Q}\boldsymbol{Q}^*)\). It projects \(\boldsymbol{A}\) into the subspace <b>orthogonal</b> to \(\boldsymbol{Q}\), removing the dominant eigendirections. It represents the leftover trace contribution after the low-rank subspace is removed.	rand_linal::hutch_pp
Is the deflated matrix \(\boldsymbol{\Delta}\) equal to the approximation error \(\boldsymbol{A} - \hat{\boldsymbol{A}}\)?	<b>No.</b> As matrices: \(\boldsymbol{\Delta} = \boldsymbol{A} - \boldsymbol{P}\boldsymbol{A} - \boldsymbol{A}\boldsymbol{P} + \boldsymbol{P}\boldsymbol{A}\boldsymbol{P}\) while \(\boldsymbol{A} - \hat{\boldsymbol{A}} = \boldsymbol{A} - \boldsymbol{P}\boldsymbol{A}\boldsymbol{P}\). These are generally <b>not equal</b>. However, their <b>traces are equal</b>: \(\operatorname{tr}(\boldsymbol{\Delta}) = \operatorname{tr}(\boldsymbol{A} - \hat{\boldsymbol{A}})\), which is all that matters for trace estimation.	rand_linal::hutch_pp
Prove that \(\operatorname{tr}(\boldsymbol{A}) = \operatorname{tr}(\hat{\boldsymbol{A}}) + \operatorname{tr}(\boldsymbol{\Delta})\) in Hutch++.	Let \(\boldsymbol{P} = \boldsymbol{Q}\boldsymbol{Q}^*\). Expand: \(\operatorname{tr}(\boldsymbol{\Delta}) = \operatorname{tr}(\boldsymbol{A}) - \operatorname{tr}(\boldsymbol{P}\boldsymbol{A}) - \operatorname{tr}(\boldsymbol{A}\boldsymbol{P}) + \operatorname{tr}(\boldsymbol{P}\boldsymbol{A}\boldsymbol{P})\). By cyclicity: \(\operatorname{tr}(\boldsymbol{P}\boldsymbol{A}) = \operatorname{tr}(\boldsymbol{A}\boldsymbol{P})\) and \(\operatorname{tr}(\boldsymbol{P}\boldsymbol{A}\boldsymbol{P}) = \operatorname{tr}(\boldsymbol{A}\boldsymbol{P}^2) = \operatorname{tr}(\boldsymbol{A}\boldsymbol{P})\) (since \(\boldsymbol{P}^2 = \boldsymbol{P}\)). So \(\operatorname{tr}(\boldsymbol{\Delta}) = \operatorname{tr}(\boldsymbol{A}) - \operatorname{tr}(\boldsymbol{A}\boldsymbol{P})\). Also \(\operatorname{tr}(\hat{\boldsymbol{A}}) = \operatorname{tr}(\boldsymbol{P}\boldsymbol{A}\boldsymbol{P}) = \operatorname{tr}(\boldsymbol{A}\boldsymbol{P})\). Therefore \(\operatorname{tr}(\boldsymbol{A}) = \operatorname{tr}(\hat{\boldsymbol{A}}) + \operatorname{tr}(\boldsymbol{\Delta})\).	rand_linal::hutch_pp
Why does Hutch++ reduce variance compared to plain Girard-Hutchinson?	Hutch++ computes the dominant trace contribution \(\operatorname{tr}(\hat{\boldsymbol{A}}) = \operatorname{tr}(\boldsymbol{Q}^*\boldsymbol{A}\boldsymbol{Q})\) <b>deterministically</b> (no randomness). The stochastic Girard-Hutchinson estimator is only applied to the <b>smaller residual</b> \(\boldsymbol{\Delta}\), which has much smaller eigenvalues. Since variance depends on the magnitude of the matrix being estimated, this dramatically reduces variance.	rand_linal::hutch_pp
What is the sample complexity of Hutch++ vs. plain Girard-Hutchinson for PSD matrices?	<b>Girard-Hutchinson:</b> \(O(1/\epsilon^2)\) matrix-vector products for \(\epsilon\) relative error. <b>Hutch++:</b> \(O(1/\epsilon)\) matrix-vector products for \(\epsilon\) relative error (with small constant failure probability). This is a substantial improvement and is <b>optimal</b> for a large class of algorithms.	rand_linal::hutch_pp
How do Hutch++ and XTrace improve the convergence rate over plain Girard-Hutchinson?	Plain Girard-Hutchinson: variance decays as \(1/m\), error as \(1/\sqrt{m}\). <b>Hutch++ and XTrace:</b> variance decays as \(1/m^2\), error as \(1/m\). This means to halve the error, you only need \(2\times\) the work instead of \(4\times\).	rand_linal::hutch_pp
What extensions of Hutch++ were proposed by Persson, Cortinovis, and Kressner?	(1) An <b>adaptive</b> version that terminates only once an error tolerance is achieved (with controllable failure probability). (2) Extension to <b>symmetric indefinite matrices</b>, with <b>additive</b> (not relative) error guarantees, since relative error is impossible when \(\operatorname{tr}(\boldsymbol{A}) = 0\).	rand_linal::hutch_pp
