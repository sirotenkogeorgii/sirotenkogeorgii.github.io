Why is a plain autoencoder not a good generative model?	A standard autoencoder has a deterministic encoder and decoder. It can reconstruct well, but the <b>latent space is unstructured</b>: sampling random latent codes usually yields meaningless outputs.	vae::motivation
What is the key idea of VAEs (Kingma & Welling, 2013)?	Make the latent space <b>probabilistic + regularized</b>, so that sampling \(z\) from a simple prior produces meaningful outputs and the model becomes a true generative model.	vae::motivation
What are the observed and latent variables in a VAE?	<b>Observed variable:</b> \(x\) (e.g., an image). <b>Latent variable:</b> \(z\) (captures hidden factors: shape, color, style, etc.).	vae::setup
What is the typical prior over latents in a VAE?	A simple prior, usually \(z \sim p(z) = \mathcal{N}(0, I)\).	vae::setup
What is the VAE sampling procedure?	(1) Sample \(z \sim p(z)\). (2) Sample \(x \sim p_\phi(x \mid z)\).	vae::setup
What is the marginal likelihood in a latent-variable model and why is it hard?	\(p_\phi(x) = \int p_\phi(x \mid z) p(z) dz\). For expressive nonlinear decoders, this integral over \(z\) is <b>intractable</b>, making direct MLE computationally infeasible.	vae::marginal_likelihood
Why do we need the posterior \(p_\phi(z \mid x)\)?	Given a datapoint \(x\), it tells us which latent codes \(z\) could plausibly have produced it. It identifies the "important" latent regions for a given observation. By Bayes' rule: \(p_\phi(z \mid x) = \frac{p_\phi(x \mid z)p(z)}{p_\phi(x)}\). But the denominator is the intractable marginal likelihood.	vae::posterior
Why is the true posterior \(p_\phi(z \mid x)\) intractable?	Because \(p_\phi(z \mid x) = \frac{p_\phi(x \mid z)p(z)}{p_\phi(x)}\), and the denominator \(p_\phi(x)\) is the intractable marginal likelihood integral.	vae::posterior
What is the variational approximation in VAEs?	Replace the intractable true posterior with a learnable approximate posterior (encoder): \(q_\theta(z \mid x) \approx p_\phi(z \mid x)\). This gives a feasible, trainable pathway \(x \to z\).	vae::posterior
What two problems does \(q_\theta(z \mid x)\) solve simultaneously in VAEs?	<b>1. Learning problem:</b> provides a tractable lower bound (ELBO) on \(\log p_\phi(x)\). <b>2. Inference problem:</b> gives an encoder from \(x\) to latent code, approximating the intractable true posterior.	vae::posterior
Which model families do NOT need posterior inference \(p(z \mid x)\)?	<b>Autoregressive models</b> (model \(p(x)\) directly), <b>Normalizing flows</b> (invertible transform, tractable likelihood), <b>Discriminative models</b> (model \(p(y \mid x)\)), <b>GANs</b> (use \(z\) for generation but don't optimize an explicit posterior). Only <b>latent-variable models</b> (VAEs, mixture models, HMMs) need posterior inference.	vae::posterior
State the ELBO bound theorem.	For any data point \(x\): \(\log p_\phi(x) \geq \mathcal{L}_{\text{ELBO}}(\theta, \phi; x)\) where \(\mathcal{L}_{\text{ELBO}} = \underbrace{\mathbb{E}_{z \sim q_\theta(z \mid x)}[\log p_\phi(x \mid z)]}_{\text{Reconstruction}} - \underbrace{D_{\mathrm{KL}}(q_\theta(z \mid x) \parallel p(z))}_{\text{Regularization}}\).	vae::elbo
Sketch the proof of the ELBO bound.	Start: \(\log p_\phi(x) = \log \int q_\theta(z \mid x) \frac{p_\phi(x,z)}{q_\theta(z \mid x)} dz = \log \mathbb{E}_{q_\theta}[\frac{p_\phi(x,z)}{q_\theta(z \mid x)}]\). Apply <b>Jensen's inequality</b> (\(\log\) is concave): \(\geq \mathbb{E}_{q_\theta}[\log \frac{p_\phi(x,z)}{q_\theta(z \mid x)}]\), which rearranges to the ELBO.	vae::elbo
What does the reconstruction term in the ELBO encourage?	\(\mathbb{E}_{z \sim q_\theta(z \mid x)}[\log p_\phi(x \mid z)]\) encourages <b>accurate recovery</b> of \(x\) from its latent code \(z\). Under Gaussian assumptions, it reduces to the familiar reconstruction (MSE) loss.	vae::elbo
What does the KL regularization term in the ELBO encourage?	\(D_{\mathrm{KL}}(q_\theta(z \mid x) \parallel p(z))\) pushes the encoder distribution to stay close to the prior \(p(z)\) (e.g., \(\mathcal{N}(0,I)\)), shaping the latent space to be smooth/continuous for meaningful sampling. <b>Key trade-off:</b> good reconstructions vs. well-structured latent space.	vae::elbo
State the joint-distribution trick that relates marginal KL to joint KL.	Define generative joint \(p_\phi(x,z) = p(z)p_\phi(x \mid z)\) and inference joint \(q_\theta(x,z) = p_{\text{data}}(x)q_\theta(z \mid x)\). Then: \(D_{\mathrm{KL}}(p_{\text{data}}(x) \parallel p_\phi(x)) \leq D_{\mathrm{KL}}(q_\theta(x,z) \parallel p_\phi(x,z))\). Intuition: comparing only marginals can hide mismatches visible in the full joint.	vae::elbo_theory
How does the joint KL decompose into modeling error and inference error?	\(D_{\mathrm{KL}}(q_\theta(x,z) \parallel p_\phi(x,z)) = \underbrace{D_{\mathrm{KL}}(p_{\text{data}}(x) \parallel p_\phi(x))}_{\text{True Modeling Error}} + \underbrace{\mathbb{E}_{p_{\text{data}}}[D_{\mathrm{KL}}(q_\theta(z \mid x) \parallel p_\phi(z \mid x))]}_{\text{Inference Error}}\). The inference error is nonneg., giving the joint KL inequality.	vae::elbo_theory
What is the ELBO gap and what does it equal?	\(\log p_\phi(x) - \mathcal{L}_{\text{ELBO}}(\theta, \phi; x) = D_{\mathrm{KL}}(q_\theta(z \mid x) \parallel p_\phi(z \mid x))\). So <b>maximizing ELBO</b> is exactly <b>reducing the inference gap</b>, pushing the variational posterior toward the true posterior.	vae::elbo_theory
Define the Gaussian VAE encoder.	\(q_\theta(z \mid x) = \mathcal{N}(z;\ \mu_\theta(x),\ \mathrm{diag}(\sigma_\theta^2(x)))\), where \(\mu_\theta: \mathbb{R}^D \to \mathbb{R}^d\) and \(\sigma_\theta: \mathbb{R}^D \to \mathbb{R}_+^d\) are deterministic neural network outputs.	vae::gaussian_vae
Define the Gaussian VAE decoder.	\(p_\phi(x \mid z) = \mathcal{N}(x;\ \mu_\phi(z),\ \sigma^2 I)\), where \(\mu_\phi: \mathbb{R}^d \to \mathbb{R}^D\) is a neural network and \(\sigma > 0\) is a (small) fixed constant.	vae::gaussian_vae
How does the ELBO specialize under a Gaussian decoder?	The reconstruction term becomes \(-\frac{1}{2\sigma^2}\mathbb{E}_{q_\theta(z \mid x)}[\|x - \mu_\phi(z)\|^2] + C\). So training becomes "regularized reconstruction": <b>scaled MSE loss</b> + <b>KL regularizer</b>. The KL between two Gaussians has a closed-form expression.	vae::gaussian_vae
Why do Gaussian VAEs produce blurry outputs?	With a Gaussian decoder + MSE training, the optimal prediction is the <b>conditional mean</b> \(\mu^*(z) = \mathbb{E}[x \mid z]\). If distinct inputs \(x \neq x'\) map to overlapping latent regions, the mean <b>averages across conflicting modes</b>, producing blurry, non-distinct reconstructions/samples.	vae::blurriness
What is the generative model (top-down) factorization in an HVAE?	With latents \(z_{1:L} = (z_1, \dots, z_L)\): \(p_\phi(x, z_{1:L}) = p_\phi(x \mid z_1) \prod_{i=2}^L p_\phi(z_{i-1} \mid z_i) \cdot p(z_L)\). Sampling: (1) \(z_L \sim p(z_L)\), (2) decode downward through layers, (3) \(x \sim p_\phi(x \mid z_1)\).	vae::hvae
What is the inference model factorization in an HVAE?	A bottom-up Markov factorization: \(q_\theta(z_{1:L} \mid x) = q_\theta(z_1 \mid x) \prod_{i=2}^L q_\theta(z_i \mid z_{i-1})\).	vae::hvae
How does the HVAE ELBO decompose into interpretable terms?	\(\mathcal{L} = \mathbb{E}_q[\log p_\phi(x \mid z_1)] - \mathbb{E}_q[D_{\mathrm{KL}}(q(z_1 \mid x) \parallel p(z_1 \mid z_2))] - \sum_{i=2}^{L-1}\mathbb{E}_q[D_{\mathrm{KL}}(q(z_i \mid z_{i-1}) \parallel p(z_i \mid z_{i+1}))] - \mathbb{E}_q[D_{\mathrm{KL}}(q(z_L \mid z_{L-1}) \parallel p(z_L))]\). Each inference conditional is regularized toward its corresponding <b>top-down</b> conditional prior.	vae::hvae
What is the core insight of hierarchical VAEs?	Stacking latent layers lets the model generate <b>progressively</b> (coarse → fine), which helps capture complex high-dimensional structure. The information penalty is distributed across layers and localized (each layer matches its neighbor).	vae::hvae
How do HVAEs conceptually connect to diffusion models (DDPMs)?	The forward noising process plays the role of a <b>fixed encoder</b> that gradually maps data to noise. The reverse denoising model is the <b>learned decoder</b> that inverts this step-by-step. Both optimize a <b>variational bound</b> on likelihood. Diffusion models inherit the progressive hierarchy idea while sidestepping HVAE weaknesses by fixing the encoding.	vae::hvae
What is Limitation 1 of "just making a flat VAE deeper"?	The variational family is still too simple. \(q_\theta(z \mid x) = \mathcal{N}(\mu_\theta(x), \mathrm{diag}(\sigma_\theta^2(x)))\) is <b>one unimodal Gaussian</b> per \(x\). Deeper networks improve \(\mu_\theta, \sigma_\theta\) but don't change the unimodal posterior family. If the true posterior is <b>multi-peaked</b>, this loosens the ELBO. Fix needs a <b>richer posterior class</b>.	vae::limitations
What is posterior collapse in VAEs?	The encoder stops putting meaningful information about \(x\) into \(z\). For many \(x\): \(q_\phi(z \mid x) \approx p(z)\), the KL term is near zero, and the decoder effectively ignores \(z\). The latent variable carries no information about the data.	vae::posterior_collapse
Explain posterior collapse via too expressive decoder.	Using \(z\) helps only if it improves reconstruction enough to justify the KL cost. A very strong decoder can model \(x\) well <b>without relying on \(z\)</b>. Then the optimizer drives KL → 0 by setting \(q(z \mid x) \approx p(z)\). Core mechanism: (1) strong decoder models \(x\) well, (2) latents cost bits via KL, (3) ELBO prefers "don't use \(z\)", (4) encoder collapses.	vae::posterior_collapse
How does posterior collapse manifest with autoregressive decoders?	An autoregressive decoder \(p_\theta(x \mid z) = \prod_i p_\theta(x_i \mid x_{<i}, z)\) can model the data from <b>local context alone</b> without using \(z\). The decoder already has a strong signal from previous tokens. Bowman et al. found training "reliably results" in zero KL divergence; weakening the decoder (e.g., word dropout) forces it to use \(z\) more.	vae::posterior_collapse
What is the information-theoretic view of posterior collapse?	The KL term is the <b>rate</b> spent to transmit information about \(x\) through \(z\). If the decoder can reconstruct well at very low rate, the optimum sits near rate 0. There can be models with the <b>same ELBO</b> but very different mutual information \(I(X;Z)\).	vae::posterior_collapse
Is posterior collapse purely an artifact of the ELBO objective?	No. Lucas et al. show that collapsed solutions can also be related to <b>local optima of the marginal likelihood itself</b>. In practice there are often two effects together: a decoder making \(z\) unnecessary, and training dynamics/local optima making collapse hard to escape.	vae::posterior_collapse
Why does the vanilla VAE not guarantee that \(z\) acts as a control signal?	The ELBO only requires the model to fit the <b>marginal data distribution</b> \(p_\theta(x) = \int p_\theta(x \mid z)p(z)dz\). It doesn't require \(z\) to determine semantic content. A collapsed model can generate the right overall population of images with \(z\) being useless. What is lost is <b>control and representation</b>, not necessarily sample quality.	vae::posterior_collapse
What techniques can force \(z\) to carry meaningful information in a VAE?	(1) <b>Weakening the decoder</b> (e.g., word dropout), (2) <b>KL annealing / free bits</b>, (3) <b>\(\beta\)-VAE</b> style changes, (4) <b>Mutual-information-promoting objectives</b>, (5) <b>Supervision/semi-supervision</b> on attributes/classes.	vae::posterior_collapse
What does "the decoder ignores \(z\)" mean concretely for a Gaussian decoder?	The output distribution parameters become nearly the same for all \(z\): \(\mu_\theta(z_1) \approx \mu_\theta(z_2)\) and \(\Sigma_\theta(z_1) \approx \Sigma_\theta(z_2)\) for many \(z_1, z_2\). So \(p_\theta(x \mid z_1) \approx p_\theta(x \mid z_2)\) — any latent vector is treated the same.	vae::posterior_collapse
How does the VAE decoder differ from a classic autoencoder decoder?	In a VAE, the decoder outputs <b>parameters of a distribution</b> (e.g., \(\mu_\theta(z), \sigma_\theta(z)\) for a Gaussian), then \(x\) is sampled from that distribution. Step 1 is deterministic (\(z \mapsto\) parameters), Step 2 is stochastic (sample \(x\)). In a classic AE, the output tensor is treated directly as the reconstruction.	vae::decoder
Can posterior collapse affect only some latent dimensions?	Yes. You may have a 32-dimensional latent vector but only 3 coordinates actually carry information, while the other 29 are effectively ignored. Collapse doesn't always mean "all of \(z\) is useless."	vae::posterior_collapse
What is the aggregated posterior and how does it appear in the ELBO decomposition?	\(q_\theta(z) = \int p_{\text{data}}(x) q_\theta(z \mid x) dx\). The expected ELBO can be rewritten as: reconstruction term \(- \mathcal{I}_q(x;z) - D_{\mathrm{KL}}(q_\theta(z) \parallel p(z))\), where \(\mathcal{I}_q(x;z) = \mathbb{E}_{p_{\text{data}}}[D_{\mathrm{KL}}(q_\theta(z \mid x) \parallel q_\theta(z))]\) is the mutual information between \(x\) and \(z\) under \(q\).	vae::elbo_theory
