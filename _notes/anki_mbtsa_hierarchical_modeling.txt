What are the three stages of a Bayesian hierarchical model?	<b>Stage I (Likelihood):</b> \(y_j \mid \theta_j, \phi \sim P(y_j \mid \theta_j)\)<br><b>Stage II (Prior on parameters):</b> \(\theta_j \mid \phi \sim P(\theta_j \mid \phi)\)<br><b>Stage III (Hyperprior):</b> \(\phi \sim P(\phi)\)<br><br>The posterior is: \(P(\phi, \theta_j \mid y) \propto P(y_j \mid \theta_j)\, P(\theta_j \mid \phi)\, P(\phi)\)	mbtsa::hierarchical::framework
Write the joint posterior for a 2-stage and a 3-stage hierarchical model.	<b>2-stage:</b><br>\(P(\theta, \phi \mid Y) \propto P(Y \mid \theta)\, P(\theta \mid \phi)\, P(\phi)\)<br><br><b>3-stage:</b><br>\(P(\theta, \phi, X \mid Y) \propto P(Y \mid \theta)\, P(\theta \mid \phi)\, P(\phi \mid X)\, P(X)\)	mbtsa::hierarchical::framework
What are the two extreme strategies for modeling multiple datasets, and their trade-offs?	<b>1. Separate Models:</b> fit independent \(\theta_i\) per dataset \(D_i\).<br>+ Captures individual differences.<br>− Noisy estimates, overfitting when \(T\) is small; no information sharing.<br><br><b>2. Fully Pooled Model:</b> fit a single \(\theta\) to all data.<br>+ Stable estimates from large data pool.<br>− Ignores inter-individual differences entirely.	mbtsa::hierarchical::extremes
What is partial pooling and how does hierarchical modeling achieve it?	<b>Partial pooling</b> is a compromise between separate and fully pooled models. Hierarchical modeling achieves it by giving each subject its own parameters \(\theta_i\), but constraining them to be drawn from a common <b>parent distribution</b> \(p(\theta \mid \beta)\) with hyperparameters \(\beta\).<br><br>This allows parameters to differ across subjects while being regularized toward the group-level distribution.	mbtsa::hierarchical::partial_pooling
Define hyperparameters and hyperpriors in Bayesian hierarchical modeling.	<b>Hyperparameters</b> \(\beta\): parameters of the prior distribution (the parent distribution from which subject-level parameters are drawn).<br><br><b>Hyperpriors</b> \(p(\beta)\): distributions placed on the hyperparameters themselves.<br><br>Structure: \(\beta \to \theta_i \to D_i\) for \(i = 1, \ldots, N\).	mbtsa::hierarchical::definitions
Write the joint distribution over data, parameters, and hyperparameters in a hierarchical model.	\(p(\beta, \{\theta_i\}_{i=1}^N, \{D_i\}_{i=1}^N) = p(\beta) \prod_{i=1}^N \left[p(\theta_i \mid \beta)\, p(D_i \mid \theta_i)\right]\)<br><br>This factorization reflects the hierarchical chain: hyperprior \(\to\) subject parameters \(\to\) data.	mbtsa::hierarchical::joint_distribution
What is the goal of hierarchical Bayesian inference? Write the posterior.	The goal is to compute the <b>joint posterior</b> of all subject-level parameters and group-level hyperparameters given data from all subjects:<br><br>\(p(\beta, \{\theta_i\}_{i=1}^N \mid \{D_i\}_{i=1}^N) = \frac{p(\{D_i\} \mid \{\theta_i\})\, p(\{\theta_i\} \mid \beta)\, p(\beta)}{p(\{D_i\})}\)	mbtsa::hierarchical::inference
Explain the information sharing mechanism in hierarchical Bayesian modeling.	The model "borrows statistical strength" across subjects:<br>(1) Data from subject \(j\) informs the posterior of \(\theta_j\).<br>(2) The posterior of \(\theta_j\) informs the group hyperparameter \(\beta\).<br>(3) The updated \(\beta\) then regularizes and improves estimates for <b>all other</b> subjects' parameters \(\theta_i\) (\(i \neq j\)).<br><br>This creates a feedback loop where every subject's data indirectly helps estimate every other subject's parameters.	mbtsa::hierarchical::information_sharing
In the delay discounting example, what is the hyperbolic discounting model and how is choice modeled?	<b>Subjective value of delayed reward:</b> \(V_{D,it} = \frac{A_{D,it}}{1 + k_i D_{it}}\), where \(k_i > 0\) is the subject-specific discount rate (higher \(k_i\) = steeper devaluation of future rewards).<br><br><b>Choice probability</b> (logistic): \(P(y_{it}=1 \mid k_i, \beta_i) = \sigma(\beta_i \Delta V_{it}) = \frac{1}{1+\exp(-\beta_i \Delta V_{it})}\)<br>where \(\Delta V_{it} = V_{D,it} - V_{I,it}\) and \(\beta_i\) is a temperature parameter.	mbtsa::hierarchical::delay_discounting
How are hierarchical priors structured in the delay discounting example?	Since \(k_i, \beta_i > 0\), model their logarithms:<br><b>Individual level:</b> \(\log(k_i) \sim \mathcal{N}(\mu_k, \sigma_k^2)\), \(\log(\beta_i) \sim \mathcal{N}(\mu_\beta, \sigma_\beta^2)\)<br><br><b>Hyperparameters:</b> \(\mu_k, \mu_\beta, \sigma_k^2, \sigma_\beta^2\)<br><br><b>Hyperpriors:</b><br>\(\mu_k \sim \mathcal{N}(\mu_{k0}, \sigma_{k0}^2)\), \(\mu_\beta \sim \mathcal{N}(\mu_{\beta 0}, \sigma_{\beta 0}^2)\)<br>\(\sigma_k^2 \sim \text{Inverse-Gamma}(a_k, b_k)\), \(\sigma_\beta^2 \sim \text{Inverse-Gamma}(a_\beta, b_\beta)\)	mbtsa::hierarchical::delay_discounting
What is the parent matrix parametrization for hierarchical models and why is it useful?	Instead of estimating \(\theta_i \in \mathbb{R}^p\) per subject (\(p \times N\) parameters), introduce:<br>- Shared <b>parent matrix</b> \(W \in \mathbb{R}^{p \times k}\) (\(k < p\))<br>- Individual vectors \(h_i \in \mathbb{R}^k\)<br>- Subject parameters: \(\theta_i = W h_i\)<br><br>Total parameters: \((p \times k) + (k \times N)\), which is much smaller than \(p \times N\) when \(k\) is small. This acts as <b>dimensionality reduction</b>.<br><br>Dependency chain: \(W, h_i \to \theta_i \to D_i\).	mbtsa::hierarchical::parent_matrix
