Why is finding eigenvalues as roots of the characteristic polynomial not an efficient approach?	Because eigenvalues are computed only by <b>numerical iterative methods</b>. Current methods for both symmetric and non-symmetric matrices have practically <b>cubic complexity</b> \(\alpha n^3\), where \(n\) is the matrix dimension.	linal::eigenvalues
State the alternative form of the spectral decomposition for a symmetric matrix (Remark 10.54).	For symmetric \(A \in \mathbb{R}^{n \times n}\) with eigenvalues \(\lambda_1, \ldots, \lambda_n\) and orthonormal eigenvectors \(x_1, \ldots, x_n\): $$A = \sum_{i=1}^n \lambda_i x_i x_i^\top$$ This decomposes \(A\) as a sum of \(n\) rank-0-or-1 matrices, where each \(x_i x_i^\top\) is the projection onto \(\operatorname{span}\{x_i\}\).	linal::spectral_decomposition
How can you interpret the map \(x \mapsto Ax\) geometrically using the alternative spectral decomposition?	As a sum of \(n\) maps: in each one, we <b>project</b> onto a line \(\operatorname{span}\{x_i\}\) (orthogonal to the others) and <b>scale</b> by the eigenvalue \(\lambda_i\).	linal::spectral_decomposition
Derive the alternative spectral decomposition \(A = \sum_{i=1}^n \lambda_i x_i x_i^\top\) from \(A = Q\Lambda Q^\top\).	Decompose \(\Lambda = \sum_{i=1}^n \lambda_i e_i e_i^\top\). Then: $$A = Q\Lambda Q^\top = Q\left(\sum_{i=1}^n \lambda_i e_i e_i^\top\right)Q^\top = \sum_{i=1}^n \lambda_i Q e_i e_i^\top Q^\top = \sum_{i=1}^n \lambda_i x_i x_i^\top$$ since \(Q_{*i} = x_i\), so \(Qe_i = x_i\).	linal::spectral_decomposition
State the Gershgorin disc theorem (Theorem 10.58).	Every eigenvalue \(\lambda\) of a matrix \(A \in \mathbb{C}^{n \times n}\) lies in a disc centered at \(a_{ii}\) with radius \(\sum_{j \neq i} |a_{ij}|\) for some \(i \in \{1, \ldots, n\}\).	linal::gershgorin
Prove the Gershgorin disc theorem.	Let \(\lambda\) be an eigenvalue with eigenvector \(x\), and let \(|x_i| = \max_k |x_k|\). The \(i\)-th equation of \(Ax = \lambda x\) gives \(\sum_{j=1}^n a_{ij}x_j = \lambda x_i\). Dividing by \(x_i \neq 0\): $$\lambda = a_{ii} + \sum_{j \neq i} a_{ij}\frac{x_j}{x_i}$$ Therefore: $$|\lambda - a_{ii}| = \left|\sum_{j \neq i} a_{ij}\frac{x_j}{x_i}\right| \leq \sum_{j \neq i} |a_{ij}|\frac{|x_j|}{|x_i|} \leq \sum_{j \neq i} |a_{ij}|$$ The last inequality uses \(|x_j|/|x_i| \leq 1\).	linal::gershgorin
How can Gershgorin discs be used as a stopping criterion for iterative eigenvalue methods?	In the <b>Jacobi method</b>, off-diagonal entries are gradually reduced. If all off-diagonal entries are less than \(10^{-k}\), then Gershgorin discs show that the diagonal entries approximate the eigenvalues with accuracy \(10^{-k}(n-1)\).	linal::gershgorin_applications
What is a diagonally dominant matrix, and how do Gershgorin discs prove it is nonsingular?	A matrix is <b>diagonally dominant</b> if \(|a_{ii}| > \sum_{j \neq i} |a_{ij}|\) for all \(i\). In this case, all Gershgorin discs exclude the origin, so 0 is not an eigenvalue, hence the matrix is nonsingular.	linal::gershgorin_applications
How do Gershgorin discs prove that the largest eigenvalue of a Markov matrix has magnitude at most 1?	All Gershgorin discs of \(A^\top\) have center in \([0,1]\) and their right edge touches 1 on the real axis (since column sums of \(A\) equal 1). This proves \(\rho(A) \leq 1\), so 1 is the largest eigenvalue in absolute value.	linal::gershgorin_applications
State the power method algorithm (Algorithm 10.61).	<b>Input:</b> \(A \in \mathbb{C}^{n \times n}\), initial \(x_0 \neq 0\).<br><b>Iterate:</b><br>1. \(y_i := Ax_{i-1}\)<br>2. \(x_i := y_i / \|y_i\|_2\)<br><b>Output:</b> eigenvalue estimate \(\lambda_1 := x_{i-1}^\top y_i\), eigenvector estimate \(v_1 := x_i\).	linal::power_method
Why does \(x_{i-1}^\top y_i\) approximate the dominant eigenvalue in the power method?	At convergence \(x_i \approx x_{i-1}\), so: $$x_{i-1}^\top y_i = x_{i-1}^\top A x_{i-1} \approx x_{i-1}^\top \lambda x_{i-1} \approx \lambda$$ since \(x_{i-1}\) is approximately the unit eigenvector.	linal::power_method
What are the advantages and disadvantages of the power method?	<b>Advantages:</b> robust (rounding errors have little effect), easily applicable to <b>large sparse matrices</b>.<br><b>Disadvantages:</b> can be slow, error and convergence rate are hard to estimate, initial choice of \(x_0\) matters significantly, does not always converge.	linal::power_method
State the convergence theorem for the power method (Proposition 10.63).	Let \(A \in \mathbb{R}^{n \times n}\) with eigenvalues \(|\lambda_1| > |\lambda_2| \geq \ldots \geq |\lambda_n|\) and corresponding linearly independent unit eigenvectors \(v_1, \ldots, v_n\). If \(x_0\) has a nonzero component in the direction of \(v_1\), then \(x_i\) converges (up to scalar multiple) to \(v_1\) and \(x_{i-1}^\top y_i\) converges to \(\lambda_1\).	linal::power_method
Prove convergence of the power method.	Express \(x_0 = \sum_{j=1}^n \alpha_j v_j\) with \(\alpha_1 \neq 0\). Then: $$A^i x_0 = \sum_{j=1}^n \alpha_j \lambda_j^i v_j = \lambda_1^i\left(\alpha_1 v_1 + \sum_{j \neq 1} \alpha_j \left(\frac{\lambda_j}{\lambda_1}\right)^i v_j\right)$$ The factor \(\lambda_1^i\) is removed by normalization. Since \(|\lambda_j/\lambda_1| < 1\) for \(j \neq 1\), the terms \((\lambda_j/\lambda_1)^i \to 0\) as \(i \to \infty\), so the vector converges to \(\alpha_1 v_1\).	linal::power_method
What determines the convergence rate of the power method?	The ratio \(|\lambda_2/\lambda_1|\). The closer this ratio is to 1, the slower the convergence. The closer to 0, the faster.	linal::power_method
What is the intuitive geometric picture of why the power method works?	Each eigenvector direction gets amplified at its own rate. The dominant eigenvector direction has the biggest stretch factor, so after repeated applications it overwhelms all others. Like an ellipse: repeated application makes the shape more elongated along the dominant axis, and almost any tracked point ends up pointing along it.	linal::power_method
Why must the starting vector \(x_0\) not be orthogonal to the dominant eigenvector in the power method?	Because \(x_0\) must contain at least a tiny component in the dominant direction. If \(x_0\) is exactly orthogonal to \(v_1\), then \(\alpha_1 = 0\) in the decomposition \(x_0 = \sum \alpha_j v_j\), and there is nothing for the dominant direction to amplify.	linal::power_method
What happens in the power method if the largest eigenvalue is negative?	The vector <b>flips sign each iteration</b>, but the <b>line</b> (direction) still converges to the dominant eigenvector direction.	linal::power_method
What happens in the power method if two eigenvalues have the same largest magnitude?	There is no single winner in the "competition" between directional components, so the method <b>may not converge</b> to a single direction.	linal::power_method
State the eigenvalue deflation proposition for symmetric matrices (Proposition 10.64).	Let \(A \in \mathbb{R}^{n \times n}\) be symmetric with eigenvalues \(\lambda_1, \ldots, \lambda_n\) and orthonormal eigenvectors \(v_1, \ldots, v_n\). Then \(A - \lambda_1 v_1 v_1^\top\) has eigenvalues \(0, \lambda_2, \ldots, \lambda_n\) and the same eigenvectors \(v_1, \ldots, v_n\).	linal::deflation
Prove the eigenvalue deflation proposition for symmetric matrices.	By Remark 10.54: \(A = \sum_{i=1}^n \lambda_i v_i v_i^\top\). Then: $$A - \lambda_1 v_1 v_1^\top = 0 \cdot v_1 v_1^\top + \sum_{i=2}^n \lambda_i v_i v_i^\top$$ This is the spectral decomposition of \(A - \lambda_1 v_1 v_1^\top\), showing eigenvalues \(0, \lambda_2, \ldots, \lambda_n\) with eigenvectors \(v_1, \ldots, v_n\).	linal::deflation
How does eigenvalue deflation work for a general (non-symmetric) matrix? (Remark 10.67)	Given eigenvalue \(\lambda\) with eigenvector \(x\), extend \(x\) to a nonsingular matrix \(S\) with \(S_{*1} = x\). Then: $$S^{-1}AS = \begin{pmatrix} \lambda & \cdots \\ 0 & A' \end{pmatrix}$$ By similarity, \(A'\) has the same eigenvalues as \(A\) with \(\lambda\)'s multiplicity reduced by one. The remaining eigenvalues can be found using \(A'\).	linal::deflation
Explain the PageRank algorithm and its connection to eigenvalues.	For \(N\) web pages, set importance \(x_i\) proportional to the sum of importances of pages linking to it: \(x_i = \sum_j \frac{a_{ij}}{b_j}x_j\), where \(a_{ij} = 1\) if page \(j\) links to page \(i\), and \(b_j\) is the number of links from page \(j\). In matrix form \(A'x = x\), so \(x\) is the eigenvector of \(A'\) for eigenvalue 1.	linal::pagerank
Why is the power method well-suited for computing PageRank?	(1) The matrix \(A'\) is <b>huge</b> (\(\approx 10^{10}\)) but <b>sparse</b>. (2) Eigenvalue 1 is <b>dominant</b> (shown via Gershgorin discs for \(A'^\top\), since column sums equal 1). (3) By Perron's theorem, the eigenvector is nonnegative. (4) Only \(\approx 100\) iterations are needed.	linal::pagerank
What are eigenvalues of graph matrices used for in graph theory? (Remark 10.67)	Eigenvalues of the <b>adjacency matrix</b> and <b>Laplacian matrix</b> reveal graph structure. They estimate the size of <b>bottlenecks</b> (vertex sets with few outgoing edges) and provide estimates on the size of <b>independent sets</b> and other graph characteristics.	linal::graph_theory
