What motivates ARMA models and what are their two components?	If residuals of a regression model on time series data are autocorrelated, the model is missing temporal structure. ARMA models capture this structure via:<br><br><b>AR (Autoregressive) part:</b> regresses the series on its own past values — captures "memory" / persistence.<br><b>MA (Moving Average) part:</b> models the current value as a function of past random shocks/innovations — captures lingering effects of specific events.	mbtsa::arma::motivation
Define the AR(\(p\)) model.	An <b>Autoregressive model of order \(p\)</b>:<br><br>\(X_t = a_0 + \sum_{i=1}^p a_i X_{t-i} + \epsilon_t\)<br><br>where \(\epsilon_t \sim \mathcal{WN}(0, \sigma^2)\). The current value depends on its own <b>past values</b>.	mbtsa::arma::definitions
Define the MA(\(q\)) model.	A <b>Moving Average model of order \(q\)</b>:<br><br>\(X_t = b_0 + \sum_{j=1}^q b_j \epsilon_{t-j} + \epsilon_t\)<br><br>where \(\epsilon_t \sim \mathcal{WN}(0, \sigma^2)\). The current value depends on past <b>error terms</b> (shocks/innovations), not past values of \(X\) itself.	mbtsa::arma::definitions
Define the ARMA(\(p,q\)) model and list its parameters.	\(X_t = c + \sum_{i=1}^p a_i X_{t-i} + \sum_{j=1}^q b_j \epsilon_{t-j} + \epsilon_t\)<br><br>Parameters: \(\theta = \{c,\; a_1, \ldots, a_p,\; b_1, \ldots, b_q,\; \sigma^2\}\)<br><br>Can be extended to include external inputs \(u_t\).	mbtsa::arma::definitions
What is the conceptual difference between AR and MA in terms of "memory"?	<b>AR models (using past \(X\)):</b> the <em>entire</em> past value influences the future — creates <b>long memory</b> where effects decay slowly (exponentially).<br><br><b>MA models (using past \(\epsilon\)):</b> only <em>random shocks</em> influence the future — creates <b>short memory</b>. After \(q\) steps, a shock is completely gone from the system.	mbtsa::arma::intuition
Why do past error terms \(\epsilon_{t-k}\) have predictive power in an MA model?	The errors are better understood as <b>shocks</b> or <b>innovations</b> — new information entering the system. Their <b>effects linger</b>:<br><br>Example: a ship enters a harbor creating a wave (\(\epsilon\)). You can't predict when the next ship arrives, but if one arrived a minute ago, you can predict the water is still choppy now.<br><br>We aren't predicting the error itself; we're predicting the <b>observed value</b> based on the fact that a specific shock recently occurred. If coefficient \(b = 0.8\), 80% of yesterday's shock still influences today.	mbtsa::arma::intuition
State the AR/MA duality: how can a finite AR(1) be represented as an infinite MA?	Any finite-order AR process can be represented as an infinite-order MA (and vice versa):<br><br>\(X_t = a_0 + a_1 X_{t-1} + \epsilon_t = a_0 \sum_{k=0}^{\infty} a_1^k + \sum_{k=0}^{\infty} a_1^k \epsilon_{t-k}\)<br><br>Derived by recursively substituting \(X_{t-1}, X_{t-2}, \ldots\). <b>Only valid if the series converges</b>, i.e., \(|a_1| < 1\).	mbtsa::arma::duality
What is the stationarity condition for AR(1), and what is the stationary mean?	For \(X_t = a_0 + a_1 X_{t-1} + \epsilon_t\):<br><br><b>Stationarity condition:</b> \(|a_1| < 1\)<br><br><b>Stationary mean:</b> \(\mathbb{E}[X_t] = \frac{a_0}{1 - a_1}\)<br><br>This follows from the infinite MA representation: the mean is \(a_0 \sum_{k=0}^{\infty} a_1^k\), a geometric series converging iff \(|a_1| < 1\).	mbtsa::arma::stationarity
How can a scalar AR(\(p\)) be written as a \(p\)-variate VAR(1)?	Define state vector \(\mathbf{X}_t = (X_t, X_{t-1}, \ldots, X_{t-p+1})^\top\). Then:<br><br>\(\mathbf{X}_t = \mathbf{a} + A\,\mathbf{X}_{t-1} + \boldsymbol{\epsilon}_t\)<br><br>where \(A\) is the \(p \times p\) <b>companion matrix</b>:<br>\(A = \begin{pmatrix} a_1 & a_2 & \cdots & a_p \\ 1 & 0 & \cdots & 0 \\ \vdots & \ddots & & \vdots \\ 0 & \cdots & 1 & 0 \end{pmatrix}\)<br><br>\(\mathbf{a} = (a_0, 0, \ldots, 0)^\top\), \(\boldsymbol{\epsilon}_t = (\epsilon_t, 0, \ldots, 0)^\top\).	mbtsa::arma::state_space
What is the stationarity condition for an AR(\(p\)) process in terms of the companion matrix?	The process is stationary iff the <b>spectral radius</b> of the companion matrix \(A\) is less than 1:<br><br>\(\max_i |\lambda_i(A)| < 1\)<br><br>where \(\lambda_i(A)\) are the eigenvalues of \(A\). For AR(1), this reduces to \(|a_1| < 1\).	mbtsa::arma::stationarity
Why do eigenvalues (not singular values) determine stationarity?	Stationarity asks about <b>asymptotic</b> behavior (\(t \to \infty\)), while singular values describe <b>single-step</b> gain.<br><br>For \(x_t = A^t x_0\): eigendecomposition gives \(A^t = P\Lambda^t P^{-1}\), so \(|\lambda_i| < 1 \Rightarrow A^t \to 0\). SVD does <b>not</b> simplify under iteration (\(A^t \neq U\Sigma^t V^\top\) for non-normal \(A\)).<br><br>A system can have \(\sigma_{\max} \gg 1\) (transient growth) yet be stable if \(|\lambda_{\max}| < 1\). Example: \(A = \begin{bmatrix} 0.5 & 100 \\ 0 & 0.5 \end{bmatrix}\) has \(\lambda = 0.5\) (stable) but \(\sigma_1 \approx 100\).	mbtsa::arma::eigenvalues_vs_svd
What is transient growth in a non-normal system, and how does it relate to deep learning?	A <b>non-normal</b> matrix (\(A^\top A \neq A A^\top\)) can have stable eigenvalues (\(|\lambda| < 1\)) but large singular values (\(\sigma_{\max} \gg 1\)), causing <b>transient growth</b> before eventual decay.<br><br>In <b>deep learning / RNNs</b>, depth = time. Non-normal weight matrices cause gradients to explode at intermediate layers (the "SVD phase") even if eigenvalues are stable. A 10-layer network may live entirely inside the transient spike.<br><br><b>Fix:</b> orthogonal initialization (\(W^\top W = I\)), where \(\sigma_i = |\lambda_i| = 1\), eliminating hidden shear.	mbtsa::arma::transient_growth
State the ACF behavior of an AR(1) process and derive it.	For zero-mean AR(1): \(X_t = a_1 X_{t-1} + \epsilon_t\)<br><br>\(\gamma(k) = a_1^k \gamma(0)\), so \(\rho(k) = a_1^k\)<br><br>The ACF <b>decays exponentially</b> to zero.<br><br><b>Derivation:</b> \(\gamma(1) = \mathbb{E}[X_t X_{t-1}] = a_1 \mathbb{E}[X_{t-1}^2] = a_1 \gamma(0)\) (since \(\mathbb{E}[\epsilon_t X_{t-1}] = 0\)). By induction: \(\gamma(k) = a_1 \gamma(k-1) = a_1^k \gamma(0)\).	mbtsa::arma::acf
State the ACF behavior of an MA(\(q\)) process.	For zero-mean MA(\(q\)): \(X_t = \epsilon_t + \sum_{j=1}^q b_j \epsilon_{t-j}\)<br><br>\(\mathrm{ACF}(k) = 0\) for all \(k > q\)<br><br>The ACF <b>sharply cuts off</b> to zero after lag \(q\). This is because for lag \(k > q\), no matching error term indices exist between \(X_t\) and \(X_{t-k}\), so all cross-product expectations vanish.	mbtsa::arma::acf
What is the PACF, and what is its key property for AR(\(p\)) processes?	The <b>Partial Autocorrelation Function</b> at lag \(k\) measures the correlation between \(X_t\) and \(X_{t-k}\) <b>after removing</b> the linear dependence on intervening variables \(X_{t-1}, \ldots, X_{t-k+1}\).<br><br>Key property: \(\mathrm{PACF}(k) = 0\) for all \(k > p\)<br><br>In an AR(\(p\)) model, the direct relationship between \(X_t\) and \(X_{t-k}\) (for \(k > p\)) is fully mediated by the first \(p\) lags.	mbtsa::arma::pacf
How do ACF and PACF patterns identify AR, MA, and ARMA models?	<table><tr><th>Model</th><th>ACF</th><th>PACF</th></tr><tr><td><b>AR(\(p\))</b></td><td>Exponential decay (tails off)</td><td>Cuts off after lag \(p\)</td></tr><tr><td><b>MA(\(q\))</b></td><td>Cuts off after lag \(q\)</td><td>Exponential decay (tails off)</td></tr><tr><td><b>ARMA(\(p,q\))</b></td><td>Tails off</td><td>Tails off</td></tr></table>	mbtsa::arma::model_identification
How are parameters estimated for AR(\(p\)) vs. ARMA(\(p,q\)) models?	<b>AR(\(p\)):</b> equivalent to <b>linear regression</b>. Construct \(y = (X_T, \ldots, X_{p+1})^\top\) and design matrix \(X\) with rows \((1, X_{t-1}, \ldots, X_{t-p})\). Solve via OLS: \(\hat{\theta} = (X^\top X)^{-1} X^\top y\).<br><br><b>ARMA(\(p,q\)):</b> the MA component makes estimation non-linear. Requires <b>numerical optimization</b>, typically maximum likelihood estimation (MLE).	mbtsa::arma::estimation
What are the main goals/uses of a fitted ARMA model?	(1) <b>Goodness-of-fit:</b> assess how well the model captures temporal structure.<br>(2) <b>Stationarity analysis:</b> determine if process properties are stable over time.<br>(3) <b>Memory and dependence:</b> orders \(p, q\) define a "memory horizon."<br>(4) <b>Hypothesis testing:</b> test significance of coefficients (e.g., \(H_0: a_i = 0\)).<br>(5) <b>Forecasting:</b> predict future values.<br>(6) <b>Control:</b> understand how to steer the system toward a desired state.	mbtsa::arma::applications
