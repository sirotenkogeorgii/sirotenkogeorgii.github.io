What is the goal of Deep Generative Modeling (DGM)?	Use a DNN to parameterize a model distribution \(p_\phi(x)\) with trainable parameters \(\phi\), and find \(p_{\phi^*}(x) \approx p_{\text{data}}(x)\).	dgm::basics
What are the three main capabilities of a deep generative model?	(1) <b>Sampling</b> from \(p_\phi(x)\) using MC or other methods. (2) <b>Density evaluation</b>: compute \(p_\phi(x')\) for a given point. (3) Note: sampling may be possible even when density is not directly computable, depending on the model class.	dgm::basics
What is the general training principle for DGMs?	Choose a discrepancy measure \(\mathcal{D}(p_{\text{data}}, p_\phi)\) and solve \(\phi^* \in \arg\min_\phi \mathcal{D}(p_{\text{data}}, p_\phi)\). Since \(p_{\text{data}}\) is not directly accessible, \(\mathcal{D}\) must be estimable from samples.	dgm::basics
What is a divergence in the statistical sense?	A \(C^2\) function \(\mathcal{D}: M \times M \to [0, \infty)\) on a differentiable manifold \(M\) satisfying: (1) \(\mathcal{D}(p,q) \geq 0\), (2) \(\mathcal{D}(p,q) = 0\) iff \(p = q\), (3) \(\mathcal{D}(p, p+dp)\) is a positive-definite quadratic form for infinitesimal displacements.	dgm::divergences
How does a divergence differ from a distance metric?	A distance metric must additionally be <b>symmetric</b> (\(\mathcal{D}(a,b) = \mathcal{D}(b,a)\)) and satisfy the <b>triangle inequality</b> (\(\mathcal{D}(a,c) \leq \mathcal{D}(a,b) + \mathcal{D}(b,c)\)). All distance metrics between distributions are divergences, but not vice versa. E.g., KL divergence is not a metric (not symmetric), but Hellinger distance is both.	dgm::divergences
Write the forward KL divergence formula.	\(D_{\mathrm{KL}}(p_{\text{data}} \parallel p_\phi) = \mathbb{E}_{x \sim p_{\text{data}}}\left[\log p_{\text{data}}(x) - \log p_\phi(x)\right] = \int p_{\text{data}}(x) \log \frac{p_{\text{data}}(x)}{p_\phi(x)} dx\)	dgm::kl_divergence
What is the mode covering effect of forward KL?	If there is a set \(A\) with \(p_{\text{data}}(A) > 0\) but \(p_\phi(x) = 0\) for \(x \in A\), the KL becomes infinite (since \(\log(p_{\text{data}}(x)/0) = +\infty\)). This strongly pressures the model to put probability mass <b>wherever the data has support</b>.	dgm::kl_divergence
How does minimizing forward KL relate to MLE?	Decompose: \(D_{\mathrm{KL}}(p_{\text{data}} \parallel p_\phi) = -\mathbb{E}_{x \sim p_{\text{data}}}[\log p_\phi(x)] + \mathcal{H}(p_{\text{data}})\). Since \(\mathcal{H}(p_{\text{data}})\) doesn't depend on \(\phi\): \(\min_\phi D_{\mathrm{KL}}(p_{\text{data}} \parallel p_\phi) \iff \max_\phi \mathbb{E}_{x \sim p_{\text{data}}}[\log p_\phi(x)]\), which is MLE.	dgm::kl_divergence
What is the empirical MLE objective in practice?	\(\hat{\mathcal{L}}_{\mathrm{MLE}}(\phi) = -\frac{1}{N}\sum_{i=1}^N \log p_\phi(x^{(i)})\), optimized with stochastic gradients / minibatches. Key: you <b>never need to evaluate</b> \(p_{\text{data}}(x)\).	dgm::kl_divergence
What is the score function of a density \(p\)?	\(s_p(x) := \nabla_x \log p(x)\). It is the gradient of the log-density with respect to \(x\).	dgm::fisher_divergence
Define the Fisher divergence.	\(D_F(p \parallel q) = \mathbb{E}_{x \sim p}\left[\|\nabla_x \log p(x) - \nabla_x \log q(x)\|_2^2\right]\). It measures how close the score (gradient) vector fields of \(p\) and \(q\) are.	dgm::fisher_divergence
Why is the Fisher divergence important for score-based models?	It is <b>invariant to normalization constants</b>: if \(q(x) \propto \tilde{q}(x)\), then \(\nabla_x \log q(x) = \nabla_x \log \tilde{q}(x)\). This makes it a natural basis for <b>score matching</b> and connects directly to diffusion modeling, where you train a model to match the data score field.	dgm::fisher_divergence
What is an \(f\)-divergence?	\(D_f(p \parallel q) = \int q(x) f\left(\frac{p(x)}{q(x)}\right)dx\), where \(f: \mathbb{R}_+ \to \mathbb{R}\) is <b>convex</b> with \(f(1) = 0\). A broad family parameterized by the choice of \(f\).	dgm::f_divergences
Give three important instances of \(f\)-divergences.	(1) <b>Forward KL:</b> \(f(u) = u \log u\). (2) <b>Jensen-Shannon:</b> \(f(u) = \frac{1}{2}[u \log u - (u+1)\log\frac{u+1}{2}]\). (3) <b>Total variation:</b> \(f(u) = \frac{1}{2}|u-1|\).	dgm::f_divergences
Write the explicit form of the Jensen-Shannon divergence.	\(D_{\mathrm{JS}}(p \parallel q) = \frac{1}{2} D_{\mathrm{KL}}(p \parallel \frac{1}{2}(p+q)) + \frac{1}{2} D_{\mathrm{KL}}(q \parallel \frac{1}{2}(p+q))\). It is <b>symmetric</b> and bounded, avoiding some unbounded KL behavior.	dgm::f_divergences
Write the explicit form of the total variation distance.	\(D_{\mathrm{TV}}(p,q) = \frac{1}{2}\int |p - q| dx = \sup_{A \subset \mathbb{R}^D} |p(A) - q(A)|\). It captures the largest possible difference in probability the two distributions can assign to any event.	dgm::f_divergences
How do Wasserstein distances differ from \(f\)-divergences?	Wasserstein distances measure the <b>minimal cost of moving probability mass</b> from one distribution to another. Unlike \(f\)-divergences (which compare density ratios), Wasserstein depends on the <b>geometry of the sample space</b> and remains meaningful even when the supports of \(p\) and \(q\) <b>do not overlap</b>.	dgm::wasserstein
What two conditions must a neural network density \(p_\phi(x)\) satisfy?	(1) <b>Non-negativity:</b> \(p_\phi(x) \geq 0\) for all \(x\). (2) <b>Normalization:</b> \(\int p_\phi(x) dx = 1\).	dgm::challenges
How do you construct a valid density from an arbitrary network output \(E_\phi(x) \in \mathbb{R}\)?	Step 1: enforce non-negativity via \(\tilde{p}_\phi(x) = \exp(E_\phi(x))\). Step 2: normalize via \(p_\phi(x) = \frac{\exp(E_\phi(x))}{Z(\phi)}\), where \(Z(\phi) = \int \exp(E_\phi(x')) dx'\) is the <b>partition function</b>.	dgm::challenges
What is the central difficulty in deep generative modeling?	Computing the partition function \(Z(\phi) = \int \exp(E_\phi(x')) dx'\) (and often its gradients) is typically <b>intractable in high dimensions</b>. Most DGM families are designed to avoid, approximate, or circumvent this cost.	dgm::challenges
Define Energy-Based Models (EBMs).	EBMs define \(p_\phi(x) = \frac{1}{Z(\phi)}\exp(-E_\phi(x))\) where \(E_\phi(x)\) is an energy function and \(Z(\phi) = \int \exp(-E_\phi(x))dx\). Lower energy = more probable. Main limitation: partition function \(Z(\phi)\) is often intractable. Connected to diffusion models via score-based ideas.	dgm::model_families
Define Autoregressive Models (ARs).	ARs factorize the joint distribution as \(p_{\text{data}}(x) = \prod_{i=1}^D p_\phi(x_i \mid x_{<i})\) using the chain rule. Each conditional is parameterized by a neural network (often Transformer). <b>Strengths:</b> exact tractable likelihood, strong density estimation. <b>Weakness:</b> slow sequential sampling.	dgm::model_families
What is the ELBO in VAEs?	\(\mathcal{L}_{\text{ELBO}}(\theta, \phi; x) = \mathbb{E}_{q_\theta(z|x)}[\log p_\phi(x|z)] - D_{\mathrm{KL}}(q_\theta(z|x) \parallel p_{\text{prior}}(z))\). First term: reconstruction accuracy. Second term: regularization toward a simple prior (usually Gaussian).	dgm::model_families
What are the strengths and limitations of VAEs?	<b>Strengths:</b> principled latent-variable modeling, widely used, foundation for diffusion models. <b>Limitations:</b> blurry/less sharp samples, posterior collapse (encoder ignores latent variables).	dgm::model_families
How do Normalizing Flows compute likelihoods?	Via the change-of-variables formula: \(\log p_\phi(x) = \log p(z) + \log|\det \frac{\partial f_\phi^{-1}(x)}{\partial x}|\), where \(f_\phi\) is an invertible mapping between a simple latent distribution and data. <b>Strengths:</b> exact tractable density. <b>Limitations:</b> restrictive architectures for bijectivity, scaling issues.	dgm::model_families
What is the GAN training objective?	\(\min_{G_\phi} \max_{D_\zeta} \mathbb{E}_{x \sim p_{\text{data}}}[\log D_\zeta(x)] + \mathbb{E}_{z \sim p_{\text{prior}}}[\log(1 - D_\zeta(G_\phi(z)))]\). Generator \(G_\phi\) maps noise to samples; discriminator \(D_\zeta\) distinguishes real from fake.	dgm::model_families
What divergence does GAN training minimize under the optimal discriminator?	Under the optimal discriminator \(\frac{p_{\text{data}}(x)}{p_{\text{data}}(x) + p_{G_\phi}(x)}\), GAN training minimizes \(2 D_{\mathrm{JS}}(p_{\text{data}} \parallel p_{G_\phi}) - \log 4\), i.e., the <b>Jensen-Shannon divergence</b>.	dgm::model_families
What distinguishes explicit from implicit generative models?	<b>Explicit models</b> directly parameterize \(p_\phi(x)\) (tractable or approximately tractable density). Examples: ARs, NFs, VAEs, DMs. <b>Implicit models</b> define the distribution only through a sampling procedure \(x = G_\phi(z)\), without a closed-form density. Example: GANs.	dgm::taxonomy
Compare the five main DGM families: EBM, AR, VAE, NF, GAN.	<b>EBM:</b> energy function, intractable partition function. <b>AR:</b> product of conditionals, exact likelihood, slow sampling. <b>VAE:</b> encoder-decoder with latent variable, approximate likelihood via ELBO. <b>NF:</b> invertible transformation, exact tractable density, architectural constraints. <b>GAN:</b> adversarial game, no explicit density, high-quality samples but unstable training.	dgm::taxonomy
How do diffusion models connect to other generative model families?	Diffusion models are <b>explicit</b> (via tractable bounds) and connect to: <b>VAEs</b> through variational training objectives, <b>EBMs</b> through score-matching (learning gradients of log-density), and <b>NFs</b> through continuous-time transformations.	dgm::taxonomy
