> Does the inner product change after changing the basis

No, the underlying inner product (the physical length of a vector and the angle between vectors) does not change when you change your basis. Just like a physical arrow's length doesn't change if you measure it with a different ruler, a vector's properties are independent of the coordinate system.

## What Actually Changes?

While the inner product's numerical value remains identical, the formula you use to calculate it changes.

* In an orthonormal basis: The inner product is simply the sum of the products of the components (the standard dot product):

$$\langle \mathbf{u}, \mathbf{v} \rangle = u_1v_1 + u_2v_2 + \dots + u_nv_n$$

* In an oblique (non-orthonormal) basis: Because your basis vectors are not mutually orthogonal or of length $1$, simply multiplying their coordinates together yields the wrong answer. You must account for the basis geometry using a metric tensor (a transformation matrix):

$$\langle \mathbf{u}, \mathbf{v} \rangle = \mathbf{u}^T A \mathbf{v}$$

## How to Calculate It

To map your inner product consistently across different bases, you use a change-of-basis matrix $P$.
If your coordinate vectors are represented by $\mathbf{x}$ and $\mathbf{y}$ in a new basis, the formula becomes:

$$\langle \mathbf{x}, \mathbf{y} \rangle = \mathbf{x}^T P^T I P \mathbf{y}$$

Where:

* $P$ is the transition matrix to the new basis.
* $I$ is the identity matrix representing the inner product in the original orthonormal basis.

So, any inner product has corresponding to it orthonormal basis?

Yes, every inner product space has an orthonormal basis.
This is guaranteed by the Gram-Schmidt orthogonalization process, which can take any arbitrary basis for a finite-dimensional inner product space and transform it into a collection of mutually perpendicular vectors with a length of exactly $1$.

## 1. Constructing the Basis

You can build an orthonormal basis from any existing basis by applying sequential geometric projections:

* Orthogonalization: Take the first vector as a reference. Subtract the parallel projections of subsequent vectors from themselves so that all remaining vectors become strictly perpendicular ($90^\circ$ apart).
* Normalization: Divide each newly perpendicular vector by its own norm (length) so that the inner product of each vector with itself equals exactly $1$. [6, 7, 8, 9, 10] 

## 2. Matrix Representation

When you use the orthonormal basis generated by that specific inner product, the matrix $A$ representing the inner product simplifies completely. The calculation reduces to the standard identity matrix:

$$\langle \mathbf{u}, \mathbf{v} \rangle = \mathbf{u}^T I \mathbf{v} = u_1v_1 + u_2v_2 + \dots + u_nv_n$$ 

## 3. Geometric Equivalence

Choosing the right orthonormal basis allows you to treat any abstract inner product space exactly like traditional Euclidean space ($\mathbb{R}^n$ with the standard dot product). All geometric relationships, angles, and distances are perfectly preserved.

## Orthonormal Basis Existence

Every finite-dimensional inner product space possesses at least one orthonormal basis, meaning you can always find a coordinate system where your specific inner product evaluates as a simple dot product.

> Does not change of basis should change the notion of unit-ball as well? In different basis it could be elongated?

No, the physical shape of the unit ball never changes. It remains a perfect, rigid sphere in the vector space because the inner product defines its true geometry.
However, its algebraic equation and its visual appearance on a standard coordinate grid will change.

## 1. Geometric Reality vs. Grid Representation

The unit ball is defined intrinsically by the inner product:

$$B = \rbrace \mathbf{v} \in V \mid \langle \mathbf{v}, \mathbf{v} \rangle \le 1 \lbrace$$ 

Since the inner product of a vector does not change when you switch bases, the exact same set of physical vectors makes up the unit ball.

## 2. Why It Looks "Elongated"

If you plot the coordinates of the unit ball on a standard, square Cartesian grid (where the axes are drawn as perpendicular and equally spaced):

* In an orthonormal basis: The equation is $x_1^2 + x_2^2 \le 1$. Plotted on the grid, it looks like a perfect circle.
* In a non-orthonormal basis: The equation becomes an ellipse, such as $2x_1^2 + 3x_1x_2 + 2x_2^2 \le 1$. Plotted on the same square grid, it looks elongated.

The elongation is an optical illusion caused by forcing a non-orthonormal coordinate system onto a standard square grid. If you skewed the grid lines to match the actual angles and lengths of your new basis vectors, the ellipse would become a perfect circle again.

## 3. Summary of Changes

| Feature | Orthonormal Basis | Non-Orthonormal Basis |
|---|---|---|
| Physical Shape | Perfect Sphere | Perfect Sphere |
| Algebraic Equation | Sum of squares ($x^2 + y^2 \le 1$) | Quadratic form ($x^T A x \le 1$) |
| Plot on Cartesian Grid | Circular / Spherical | Elongated Ellipse / Ellipsoid |

Yes. If you plot the coordinates on a standard, square graph paper, the exact same set of vectors will look like a perfect circle/ball in one coordinate system and an elongated ellipse/ellipsoid in another.

Here is exactly why this happens:

## 1. The Algebraic View

The unit ball is the set of vectors where $\langle \mathbf{v}, \mathbf{v} \rangle \le 1$.

* In an Orthonormal Basis ($B_1$): The inner product is a simple sum of squares. If a vector has coordinates $(x, y)$, the boundary equation is:

$$x^2 + y^2 = 1$$ 

On standard graph paper, this plots as a perfect circle.
* In a Non-Orthonormal Basis ($B_2$): The basis vectors might be skewed or have different lengths. The exact same inner product now requires a matrix $A$ to account for this geometry. If the same physical vector has new coordinates $(u, v)$, the boundary equation becomes a quadratic form:

$$au^2 + buv + cv^2 = 1$$

On that exact same standard graph paper, this formula plots as an ellipse.

## 2. The Paper Analogy

Imagine drawing a perfect circle on a sheet of stretchy rubber.

* The circle is the unit ball. It is a fixed, physical reality.
* Choosing a basis is like drawing a grid on the rubber.
* If you draw a standard square grid ($B_1$), the circle looks like a circle relative to the grid lines.
* If you stretch the rubber diagonally and draw a new grid ($B_2$), the physical circle hasn't changed, but relative to your new, distorted grid lines, the boundary crosses the coordinates in the shape of an ellipse.

## 3. Summary

The "unit ball" is the physical object. The "ellipse" is just how that object looks when its coordinates are forced onto a flat, un-skewed computer screen or piece of paper. Under the same inner product, the physical vectors never change; only their numerical descriptions stretch or shrink.

> So, a unit ball is actually "a ball" only in orthonormal basis, in non-orthonormal basis it is "a unit ellipse"?

Strictly speaking, yes, geometrically. It is only a "ball" in the traditional sense when viewed through the lens of an orthonormal basis.
To be completely accurate, we must separate the physical object from its coordinate name:

## 1. Intrinsic Reality vs. Extrinsic Label

* The Object: It is always a perfect sphere relative to its own inner product. The inner product defines what "distance" means, so every point on the boundary is exactly one unit away from the center.
* The Visual Representation: If you look at it using a non-orthonormal basis, the coordinates warp. On a standard, square Cartesian grid, the object visually deforms into an ellipse.

## 2. The Definition Shifts

Because of this coordinate warping, mathematicians use specific language depending on the context:

* In its own space: It is always called the unit ball because the metric of that space defines it as perfectly round.
* Relative to a standard Euclidean space: If you map that space onto standard Euclidean coordinates ($\mathbb{R}^n$ with the standard dot product), it maps directly to a bounded ellipsoid.

## 3. A Visual Example

Imagine a basis where the first basis vector $\mathbf{e}\_1$ has a true physical length of $1$, but the second basis vector $\mathbf{e}\_2$ has a true physical length of $2$.

* The vector $(1, 0)$ has a length of $1$.
* The vector $(0, 0.5)$ also has a length of $1$ (since $0.5 \times 2 = 1$).

If you plot the points $(1,0)$ and $(0, 0.5)$ on standard, evenly spaced graph paper, the boundary must pass through $1$ on the x-axis but only $0.5$ on the y-axis. The resulting shape drawn on your paper is a squished ellipse, even though both points are exactly "one unit" away from the center according to that space's metric.
